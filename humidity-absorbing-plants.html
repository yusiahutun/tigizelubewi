8 Humidity-Absorbing Houseplants That Reduce Moisture Naturally

 Posted on February 27, 2024  |  4 minutes  |  745 words  |  Jenniffer Sheldon

Warm, humid summers can make our homes feel as though they're full of moisture. The same also goes for winter when mustiness and winter mildew are created by extra humidity and little ventilation.

However, instead of running a dehumidifier all day long, introducing certain indoor plants to your home can help to make a difference in regulating humidity levels.

"Plants are natural dehumidifiers because they absorb water from their surroundings through their leaves and release moisture back out through transpiration," explains the plant experts at Jay Scotts. "This process helps regulate humidity levels and create fresh air in any space."

How do plants absorb humidity?

It's through a process called 'foliar acceptance'. This is the ability to absorb dewdrops, fog, and other kinds of moisture via leaves. We sometimes encourage this ourselves by misting our houseplants, rather than watering them at their roots.

What types of plants reduce humidity in the home?

Plants that originate from humid countries are more likely to evolve the adaptation to absorb damp air, but some plants in arid areas with little rainfall may also be able to process moisture in this manner, such as cacti.

Keep reading to discover eight houseplants that reduce humidity in your home...

1. Peace lily

OsakaWayne Studios//Getty Images

Regardless of being watered, this much-loved houseplant will also absorb dampness from the air through its leaves. It also requires little sunlight to flourish, making it perfect for most areas of the home.

However, as a member of the lily family, it's toxic to people and pets if ingested. As such, keep this beautiful plant displayed out of reach of children and curious animals.

2. Boston fern

automidori//Getty Images

Boston ferns are a type of epiphyte – that is, a plant that grows on the surface of another plant and derives its moisture and nutrients from air, rain or water. Other epiphytes include types of moss, fern and cacti.

They require indirect sunlight as well as damp soil to thrive. In winter, keep Boston ferns out of drafts and in a warmer room of the house.

3. Palms

Olena Ruban//Getty Images

Palm varieties found in damp, tropical regions of the world absorb humidity through their leaves. Lady’s palm, bamboo palm, areca palm and reed palm are all a few examples of varieties you can pick to display inside your home.

Palms are easy to take care of, but still need regular watering to keep their soil damp. They can live in direct daylight, but if it becomes excessive, their leaves can burn.

4. Orchids

Dmitriy Sidor//Getty Images

Orchids are another type of epiphyte and acquire nutrients and moisture from the air around them.

A stylish choice for the home, there are plenty of different colours and varieties to choose from.

5. Spider Plant

Veena Nair//Getty Images

Spider plants have long been a popular choice when it comes to choosing indoor plants, largely thanks to their leafy looks and low-maintenance care routine.

Not only are they a great natural dehumidifier, they're also great for people with busy routines and those with a lack of light in their homes. Spider plants are content in low-light rooms and shady spots, and make a great choice for kitchens and bathrooms.

6. Tillandsia

athima tongloom//Getty Images

Tillandsia plants can survive on the nutrients and moisture from the air around them, absorbing everything they need through their leaves.

Sit them in a bright, sunny spot and water a couple of times a week. You'll know when you're under-watering the plant, as its leaves become gradually more bowl-shaped.

7. Cacti

Hanneke Vollbehr//Getty Images

Cacti flourish in deserts and barely ever need to be physically watered. Instead, they grasp and retain water from their surrounding air to decrease humidity naturally.

8. English ivy

Kseniya Ovchinnikova//Getty Images

A hanging planter or raised plant pot makes a great home for English ivy, a wonderful trailing plant. It will thrive in environments with 40% humidity or higher.

It doesn't require much sunlight, just regular watering and plenty of room for its leaves to trail freely. If your home gets too dry, mist the leaves with water.
