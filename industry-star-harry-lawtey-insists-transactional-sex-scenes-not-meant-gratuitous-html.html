Industry star Harry Lawtey insists his 'transactional' sex scenes are not meant to be gratuitous

 Posted on May 31, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  555 words  |  Martina Birk

Harry Lawtey insists his risqué sex scenes in hit drama Industry were 'just another day at the office' and should be viewed as a symbolic 'exchange of power' rather than unjustified, over-the-top exposition. 

Set in the heart of Canary Wharf - London's sprawling financial hub - the show centers on a group of ambitious young graduates and their attempts to forge a career in the fast-paced, cut-throat world of investment banking - by any means necessary. 

Lawtey plays the bullish, cocky and frequently coke-addled Robert Spearing, an Oxford graduate with Welsh working class roots and a seemingly unquenchable appetite for sex. 

But he believes the show's frequent and indeed graphic illustrations of sex fulfill an important role in symbolizing its power-hungry characters. 

Speaking to The Times, Lawtey, 27, admitted he was prompted to consider their relevance when a fan recently commented 'that it’s interesting that there’s so much sex in a show that is about money.'

Here we go: Lawtey plays the bullish, cocky and frequently coke-addled Robert Spearing, an Oxford graduate with Welsh working class roots and an unquenchable appetite for sex

'And I hope the sex scenes don’t feel gratuitous,' he added. 'They are all an exchange of power. They’re very transactional, in a way. And that’s not to say that they’re not romantic. You can have both.' 

One memorable scene from its first series - the show was recently renewed for a third - sees Spearing ejaculate on a mirror, but the actor has since laughed it off as part of the job. 

'Just another day at the office,'  he joked. 

The BBC financial drama has shocked viewers with its steamy sex scenes, with one viewer calling for 'more banking, less bonking'.

During its first series, which aired in 2020, the characters were seen performing oral sex, stripping down to lingerie and secretly watching porn, while relatively little time was spent in the office.
