Undercover cops dressed in hi-viz ambush an alleged meth dealer in Western Australia

 Posted on August 30, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  567 words  |  Martina Birk

Dramatic footage has emerged of police swooping on armed meth dealers as part of a sting operation that resulted in charges against 13 people.

The video shows authorities dressed in hi-viz workwear dragging men from cars and tackling them to the ground in the three-day blitz that took place across suburbs of Perth.

Operation Bowerbird targeted street-level ice dealers who were allegedly using the Telegram app to sell their wares via encrypted messages. 

The footage shows police running to a black sedan at a BP petrol station, before dragging a man in a bright red t-shirt onto the bitumen of a carpark (pictured)

Officers posed as prospective customers for drug deals, the West Australian said. 

Vision of the arrests showed police disguised in hi-viz vests and shirts swarming around a grey hatchback as the driver lays on the ground restrained by officers - before another man was plucked from the car by police.

Officers were also seen running to a black sedan at a BP petrol station, before dragging a man in a bright red t-shirt onto the bitumen.

A small bag with a white substance was allegedly found in the hatchback, while more vision revealed officers restraining another man with long hair. 

Police charged 13 people with 28 offences, which included offering to sell or supply a prohibited drug, possessing a prohibited drug and possessing a prohibited weapon. 

Officer-in-charge Detective Senior Sergeant Mark Emmett from the Drug and Firearm squad told the West Australian the dealers cannot hide behind the veil of anonymity. 

'They need to watch out because we are always around, we are always targeting people and they could end up before the courts,' he said.  

'We have a number of investigative techniques that we apply and we are able to deal with people who are conducting illicit activity on the internet through different platforms.

'We can engage different people in an open-market scenario on a platform and they don't ever know if they are speaking to a police officer or not.'

He said some substances sold on the street had other ingredients in them, like sugar, ripping buyers off by hundreds of dollars.

Det Snr Sgt Emmett added there was a possibility drug users could ingest more dangerous substances added to the mix. 

He also said some of the arrested drug dealers were armed and warned their customers about the dangers of mixing with criminals with guns. 

It comes after The Sunday Times revealed earlier this year gangs were selling ice in sophisticated ways using the Telegram app organised by a group called 'the Circuit.'
