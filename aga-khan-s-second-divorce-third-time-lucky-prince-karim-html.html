Third time lucky for Aga Khan?

After years of wrangling, the Aga Khan’s second divorce — revealed by court papers to have cost him £54 million — opens an intriguing possibility for the leader of the world’s Ismaeli Muslims and one-time playboy.

At the age of 74, will he contemplate a third marriage?

His separation from his second wife, former Princess Gabriele zu Leiningen, lasted longer than their marriage, and throughout much of this time his most frequent companion has been British-based divorcee Beatrice von der Schulenburg.

Next in line? Newly divorced Prince Karim Aga Khan with Countess Beatrice Von Der Schulenburg

She is the daughter of Sixties folk singer Baron Frederik van Pallandt — half of the Danish singing duo Nina & Frederik.

He later went into drug- trafficking and was shot dead in the Philippines in 1994, along with his Filipina common-law wife, apparently victims of a narcotics syndicate.

Beatrice, whose former husband Jeffrey was the boss of a City recruitment company, has kept a low profile since being spotted with the Aga on a yacht off the Costa Smerelda in Sardinia.

Old friends of the Aga Khan say there is little need for him to enter into matrimony again. He has three grown-up children by his first wife, the former British model Sally Croker-Poole, and a son, Aly, by his second.

The announcement of a deal  in Paris on Friday to end the divorce battle with his statuesque German-born second wife surprised many, who thought  he was in no hurry to reach  a settlement.

Moving on: The Aga Khan's separation from his second wife, former Princess Gabriele zu Leiningen, lasted longer than their marriage

The divorce was begun in the High Court in London before switching across the Channel to France. The racehorse-owning Aga, who is worth £6 billion and has homes on five continents, lives mainly at his estate near Chantilly, north of Paris.

It was reported that he moved there in the hope of getting a better divorce settlement than he would have expected in Britain.

In the event, the deal is remarkably similar to the cost of his first divorce in 1995.

Princess Gabriele moved to Britain and lived quietly near Ascot when the divorce was first switched to London. But she and her son moved to Switzerland a year ago.

 

Madonna's royal love-note row

Royal Mail: Madonna on the red carpet for W.E at the Venice Film Festival

The news that she was making a film about the abdication crisis was received with a chilly silence by the Royal Family.

And when Madonna likened herself to the American adventurer Wallis Simpson, with whom she felt a kin-ship, the reservations only deepened.

But the pop queen reveals that there were some among her well-connected critics who were just as keen to exploit her.

It came in the early days of her film  project when she was scouring the country for letters between Wallis, Edward VIII and other central figures in the drama.

Madonna’s movie, W.E., about the love affair that imperilled the monarchy, gets its premiere later this month at the BFI London Film Festival.

Her screenplay is based on these letters. But many of them were in private collections and, according to Madonna, some of their owners asked her for money for them, which she refused.

She declines to name names, but asked if she had paid anyone, Madonna says: ‘No.’

However, she does single out for praise former Harrods owner Mohammed Al Fayed.

He acquired the house in Paris — complete with contents — where the Duke and Duchess of Windsor lived, and allowed Madonna to see his collection of royal letters without any strings.

If she wasn’t already, Kate Middleton has been firmly established as part of London’s fabric. The first pub has opened in the capital bearing the name Duchess of Cambridge since her wedding to Prince William.

‘One of our directors is a good friend of Prince William’s private secretary [Jamie Lowther-Pinkerton], so he thought it would be great to name the pub after the new Duchess of Cambridge,’ says Simon Waind, 32, landlord of the Shepherd’s Bush boozer. 

‘There has been talk of Kate visiting the pub — she would be very welcome.’

 

For writer William Cash, restoring his family home, Upton Cressett Hall in Shropshire, to its former glory has been a labour of love.

After four years of graft — not to mention writing of cheques — the hard work appears to have paid off.

The 15t-century moated manor has been nominated in three categories for Hudson’s Heritage Awards, the Oscars of the stately home business.

The house was bought by Cash’s Eurosceptic Tory MP father Bill in 1971, and during its 500-year history has played host to such figures as Edward V and Baroness Thatcher.

More recently, Elizabeth Hurley was a guest at a dinner there to celebrate the completion of the restoration and the opening of the Upton Cressett Foundation, a literary retreat for writers.

‘People call up and want to stay in the Elizabeth Hurley suite for their honeymoon, but I say it’s actually called the Library Bedroom and will stay that way,’ says Cash, 44.

 

What a Carry On by Sally

Knee-jerk reactions: Sally Farmiloe got a little too into character while acting in Carry On Brighton

Actor Daniel Day-Lewis spent weeks in a wheelchair to portray crippled Christy Brown for the Oscar-winning film My Left Foot. Such thespian dedication has rubbed off on Sally Farmiloe.

Sally, former squeeze of Lord Archer, has just completed the run of Carry On Brighton, a homage to the fruity Carry On films, at Hampstead’s Pentameters Theatre.

The script required her burka-wearing character, Fatima, to knee Dead Ringers actor Mark Perry in the groin. On the final night, Sally’s over-enthusiastic knee action apparently poleaxed Perry, triggering gasps from the audience.

‘I did advise Mark to wear a cricketer’s box and told him to stand upstage so the audience didn’t actually have to see my knee going into his wotsits. He did neither,’ says a repentant Sally.

‘I had no intention of hurting him. Afterwards, he didn’t speak to me at the cast party.’

A fully recovered Perry assures me he is taking it on the chin, so to speak.

Nobel prize-winning writer V. S. Naipaul and his wife Khannum are in mourning — for their cat.

The 79-year-old author was do devoted to black-and-white moggie Augustus, who appeared on some of his book covers, that he placed an announcement in the births, marriages and deaths page of The Times. It read: ‘Our much beloved Augustus Naipaul passed away on 26th September 2011 and is sadly missed by V. S. Naipaul, K. Naipaul and Sandra, housekeeper.’

Naipaul’s literary agent Luke Ingrams tells me: ‘He was named after the first Roman emperor; the author said that with Augustus in residence, the house was no longer a republic.’
