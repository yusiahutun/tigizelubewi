How &quot;Lessons in Chemistry&quot; Reinvents Classic 1950s Costuming

 Posted on August 3, 2024  |  5 minutes  |  942 words  |  Martina Birk

There’s always a sense of hesitation when one of the most popular books of a particular moment gets adapted to the screen. Will they nail the details? Will they capture what we loved about it? So far, it seems that Apple TV+’s new Lessons in Chemistry, based on the novel by Bonnie Garmus, gets it right.

In the miniseries, we witness the journey of Elizabeth Zott, played by Brie Larson, across the 1950s and ’60s, as she leaves a career in science and becomes a beloved cooking show host, ultimately using her platform on Supper at Six to educate housewives about—you guessed it—lessons in chemistry.

We’ve seen midcentury fashion so many times onscreen, but Lessons in Chemistry offers a fresh perspective. We caught up with costume designer Mirren Gordon-Crozier to learn more about her tricks of the trade.

Tell me about your process.

I had read the book, so I knew where the characters would eventually end up. I am an avid cookbook person, and I am obsessed with research images and color swatching and trying to source deadstock fabrics and prints and stuff. In this case, Elizabeth goes through many stages in her life, so the lookbook I ended up creating was about 120 pages for all the characters. And from there we go on to our fittings. Each fitting was four or five hours long. Brie had 12 changes every episode, so it really added up.

Courtesy of Mirren Gordon-Crozier

You’ve worked with Brie Larson many times before, including on Unicorn Store, The Glass Castle, and Short Term 12. How was this time different?

Working with Brie is my absolute favorite. First of all, I know her body type and what she likes and doesn’t like. And there’s a certain trust between the two of us. Having that shorthand relationship is priceless, really. We’ve done a variety of shows together. Unicorn Store was wearing a PVC rain jacket covered in stickers. Glass Castle is set in the ’80s. Short Term 12 was mostly jeans and T-shirts. So doing this was the culmination of everything coming together. And Brie loves fashion, so we really enjoyed it.

How do we see her character evolve?

When we start with Zott, we see her as a lab tech. It was the early ’50s, which was still very ’40s. The secretaries are dressed really on-trend, but Zott’s character was still wearing her slightly more ’40s-esque work clothes. She’s always wearing her lab coat, and the other women in the office are prim and proper, and they’re there to find their husbands, and they’re in pastels and perfectly accessorized outfits. Right away, you notice the contrast. And when Zott goes home, she oftentimes will wear her jeans, which wasn’t very common back then.

Courtesy of Mirren Gordon-Crozier

As we watch Zott transition from lab tech to television host, how does her style change?

In the beginning, it’s very “off the rack,” and as the show progresses she goes into a more couture lab coat. A lot of those silhouettes were based on Dior and designers of the time who really knew how to use beautiful lines and cuts without too much distraction or frivolity. That progression of her costume was one of the most interesting challenges I’ve ever had to do. We make the point that she’s one of the first women to wear pants on national television at the time, which was quite shocking. She starts to have fun with her wardrobe.

Courtesy of Mirren Gordon-Crozier

So we can witness her character development as her onscreen lab coats evolve over time.

Yes, in the beginning she’s quite utilitarian. And as the show moves on, the lab coats change. We made a special lab coat for the Christmas episode, and it has this amazing Dior sort of overcoat silhouette, which was fantastic, thanks to the tailors on our team.

Did you source actual 1950s clothes and use those for the costuming?

For Zott, nothing was sourced. For the other characters, we did a combination of building and renting. We had all the best costume houses at our fingertips, and it would’ve been a shame not to use them. Western Costume has genuine garments from that era in great condition. They have an amazing deadstock fabric collection from the ’50s. Knowing it was from that era makes it better and more genuine. We would buy old patterns from the ’50s and re-create them.

So do we see a literal interpretation of the 1950s, or your creative interpretation of it?

It’s very literal in the sense that I researched endlessly. I’m very true to the early ’50s, which is really a culmination of the late ’40s.

Courtesy of Mirren Gordon-Crozier

What was the hardest costume to get right?

Once again, the lab coats. Finding the right white is so much harder than you think. On camera, bright white is no good. Creams can easily look too dull. It has to be the right cotton twill. We tried multiple, multiple times. Sometimes it wouldn’t hang right, or was too bulky, or too thin. I think I drove my shoppers crazy looking for 500 swatches of white cotton fabric. And once we found it, it’s like finding the right pair of jeans—a very simple concept, but finding the right weight and fit is challenging. The lab coat is one item, but it could go badly if it wasn’t in the right weight and tone.
