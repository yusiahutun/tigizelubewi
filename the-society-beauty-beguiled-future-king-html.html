Sheila Loughborough was the vivacious Australian who dazzled London’s beau monde and caught the eye of Prince Albert, the future King George VI. Here Robert Wainwright tells the story of their little known but controversial romance and shares their previously unpublished letters 

Sheila in her high-society heyday

In march 1918, Sheila Loughborough and Prince Albert met at a dance at Stafford House (now Lancaster House), near St James’s Palace in London. Its rococo interior was so grand that Queen Victoria, when she attended a dinner there many years before, had remarked on arrival: ‘I have come from my house to your palace.’

Prince Albert, known as Bertie, and his brother Edward, the Prince of Wales, circled the room as they were introduced to guests. Eventually the two princes reached a group of women in which Freda Dudley Ward (Edward’s secret lover) had positioned herself. Sheila Loughborough (who was married to Lord Loughborough) was standing next to Freda in a golden gown. They had not met before but Albert fell for Sheila’s wide eyes and insisted on a dance. Neither of them knew that this chance meeting would lead to a lifelong friendship, full of secrets.


'My Dear Sheila... I do so miss you...
I shall look forward to the day you return'

Sheila recalled later that, as the princes approached, she had asked Lady Rosemary Leveson-Gower, who was standing with her, what she should say and do. ‘Curtsey to the ground, call them sir and treat them like dirt,’ Rosemary replied. Sheila danced with both princes. She would also note in her memoir, ‘It was an enjoyable evening, and I told them my grandmother was a kangaroo!’

Edward’s relationship with Freda soon became all-consuming. They found ways of being together almost every day or, if they could not manage that, would scrawl gushing, embarrassing love notes to each other. And Bertie – quiet, hesitant and afflicted with a crippling stammer – was shadowing his elder brother, including losing his virginity to a Parisian prostitute as Edward had done. It was not surprising then that Bertie and Sheila, Freda’s best friend, morphed into a private social unit with Edward and Freda.

Smoking nonchalantly between Prince Albert, far left, and Prince Edwards

They called themselves the 4 Do’s, a nickname dreamed up by Edward, perhaps as a rebellion against being told what they couldn’t do – a secret club within their very public existence, marked with a teddy bear on the dashboard of Bertie’s car given to him by Sheila. They were dangerous, exciting liaisons for two young princes challenging social expectation; exploring their independence with two unhappily married women.

The 4 Do’s attended the theatre and music halls; they arranged dinner with friends who knew of the liaisons, and mingled carefully in public at dances and parties in the grand houses and ballrooms of West London. They even stole time at Freda and Sheila’s homes, sometimes under the noses of their husbands, who were either unaware of or ignored their wives’ indiscretions.

Sheila’s relationship with the gentle Bertie had developed beyond mere friendship. There was a mutual attraction, and from Sheila’s perspective a welcome distraction, as her marriage to Lord Loughborough (Loughie), who was a compulsive gambler, continued to disintegrate.

In the last days of May 1919, she waved goodbye to her mother, who finally returned to Sydney after five years abroad. Sheila was now alone with two children under the age of two, the rest of her family a six-week journey away by ship on the other side of the world.

As Sheila wrestled with her future and wondered at her improbable relationship with the second in line to the British throne, Lady Cunard invited her to lunch. It was an intimate affair held at Kenwood House in Hampstead, owned by the Guinness family, its walls lined with works by the great masters – Rembrandt, Vermeer, Turner, Reynolds, Gainsborough and Constable. ‘I sat opposite Serge Obolensky [a Russian prince],’ she would write of that day. ‘He only had to look at a woman for her to fall in love with him. He looked at me. My heart stood still.’

A portrait of Sheila, by then Lady Milbanke, by Simon Elwes from the late 1930s

A week later Sheila and Loughie went to Scotland for a month to try to rekindle their marriage. When they returned to London, Sheila’s thoughts turned again to Serge. Bertie had been sent by his father to study at Trinity College, Cambridge.

Their relationship was cooling, but they continued to see each other when he was in London. Despite their efforts at secrecy it was only a matter of time before their affair and the liaison between Prince Edward and Freda Dudley Ward was reported back to Buckingham Palace. By April 1920 the activities of Edward and Albert had reached the ears of King George and Queen Mary.

King George decided to confront his errant sons. The issue was far more serious with the Prince of Wales, given that he was heir to the throne, but he was on his way to Australia for an official tour. So the king summoned his second son on 7 April and offered a tantalising inducement, as Bertie told his brother in a letter written the next morning. ‘He is going to make me Duke of York on his birthday provided that he hears nothing more about Sheila and me!!!’

After he had received his brother’s laments, some six weeks later, Edward wrote this outburst to Freda:

May 22, 11pm at sea

Christ! How I loathe and despise my bloody family as Bertie has written me three long, sad letters in which he tells me he’s getting it in the neck about his friendship with poor little Sheilie & that TOI ET MOI came in for it too!! But if HM thinks he’s going to alter me by insulting you he’s making just about the biggest mistake of his silly useless life…’

Edward, who would stand by his word and reject his father’s demands for an end to the relationship with Freda, had calmed down two days later when he penned a second letter. He and Freda had known about the relationship that had developed between ‘Sheilie and Ob [Obolensky]’ for several months – so his initial indignation on Bertie’s behalf had tempered considerably by his second letter.

May 24

You & I both happen to know that neither of them really mean anything to each other at all, do they sweetheart?…From what he tells me S & L [Loughie] are living on top of a hotter volcano than ever & anyone in love with her is sitting on top with them! We both of us know she really loves Ob so surely it would be better for Bertie to drift away from it all & just be one of Sheilie’s friends…

Sheila, Edwards, Freda and Bertie - 'the 4 do's' - during a visit to Sheila's WInchester home Lankhills in 1919

Bertie knew that he had to obey his father. Sheila was married, and even if she left Loughie, his parents would not approve of marriage to a divorcée. It would come to a head on the night of 2 June 1920, when King George hosted his traditional pre-Derby male-only dinner at Buckingham Palace for members of the Jockey Club. The next day was his 55th birthday, when he intended to appoint his second son as Duke of York. That evening Bertie, who had travelled down from Cambridge, wrote his father a note in which he said he was proud to accept the title and hoped he would live up to it, adding ‘I can tell you I fulfilled your conditions to the letter and that nothing more will come of it.’

Even though the prince would complain to his brother about being spied on every time he went out during that summer, there would be one last dance with his former lover. After signing his acceptance letter, Bertie went off to dinner at the palatial home of Lord and Lady Farquhar with his mother Queen Mary, his sister Princess Mary and his younger brothers George and Henry.

There were 76 guests at three tables decorated with sweet peas to celebrate the Farquhars’ silver wedding anniversary. They were joined after dinner by a further 150 guests, among them Sheila Loughborough, as the evening turned to dancing.

Queen Elizabeth and King George VI on their wedding day in 1923

The Duke of Windsor and Wallis Simpson on their wedding day in June 1937

Despite the presence of his mother and his commitment to his father, Bertie could not resist dancing with the woman he had agreed to avoid. As they took their turn around the dancefloor, they both noticed a young woman standing in the doorway. She seemed young and vulnerable – a debutante alone in a room filled with society veterans. She was the 19-year-old Elizabeth Bowes-Lyon.


'Bertie tells me he's getting it in the neck about his friendship with Sheila'

In a desperate last attempt to save her marriage, Sheila persuaded Loughie to go back with her and the children to Australia for a year. When an envelope stamped with the royal seal arrived she opened it carefully. She then stored it among her most precious belongings, with the others that followed from Bertie.

December 20

My Dear Sheila,

Whenever I go into a ballroom I always look around the room hoping to see you, as I know there is somebody missing, and it is so so sad not seeing you, and I do so miss you…I shall look forward to the day when you return and I hope to be able to welcome you back once more.

My very best love for you Sheila

Yours ever sincerely
Albert

Sheila and Loughie prepare to board a ship back to London in 1923, after two years in Australia

Sheila with her second husband Sir John Milbanke on their wedding day in 1928

He had promised to give her up, but this letter made it clear that he was struggling to forget her. There seemed to be a longing and regret that they could never be together and that their affair had been cut short, not by them but by his father’s demands.

August 21st, 1921

My Dearest Sheila,

Thank you ever so much for your letter of 16th June. I am so glad you received mine safely…No, I am not engaged or married yet!! Does it surprise you at all? I don’t think I ever shall be, anyhow not for some long time to come. It is all so difficult and tricky these days here, and there seems to be more and more gossiping old women than before. I have seen very little of Freda lately who has been out of London and away for some time.

I am so sorry to hear you say you have no plans for returning to England yet. We are all waiting patiently for you!! I do hope things are going easier for now and that you are not so worried as before.

I often think of the old days in 1919; they seem a very, very long way away now, but hold many memories. Your baby bear mascot is still on the car guiding me straight as before!! Do please write again soon and tell me more about yourself, as you know how I want to know.

Ever yours very sincerely

Albert

Sheila with sons Peter and Tony in 1930

It would take more than a year for the Loughboroughs to pack up and head back to Europe, not as a reconciled couple as Sheila had hoped, but disenchanted and together only for appearances and the sake of the children.

York Cottage, Sandringham

December 22, 1922

My Dear Sheila,

I was so glad to receive your letter of November 8th this morning. I was just going to write to tell you how very glad I am to hear that you are definitely coming home this next April with all your family which means, I suppose, that you are coming for good. Freda told me at a dance the other night all about it and it will be nice seeing you again after all this time – two years, as you say.

Yes I think things are more or less unchanged here and we all do the same things as we did before. But I have not seen much of David [the name by which Prince Edward was known to his family] and Freda lately as we are moving and hunting in different places and the days do not always fit in. Life is just as strenuous, plenty of work. We fit in hunting between stunts when we can, which is a good life and every moment is occupied.

I am down here with the family for a few days for Christmas, after which I hope to get a good month hunting. I have given David your love and he sends his. Please forgive this very dull letter. I shall be looking forward to your return with great expectation.

Ever yours sincerely,

Albert

He missed one piece of news, perhaps because of a continual longing for Sheila or because it was too personal. Albert had proposed a second time to Elizabeth Bowes-Lyon who, yet again, rejected him because she regarded their relationship as a friendship. He would try once more in February 1923. This time she accepted and they married on 26 April.


'My Dear Sheila... how very glad I am to hear that you are definitely coming home'

Two weeks later Sheila arrived back in London with her husband and their two sons. Sheila summed up their time in Australia succinctly and sadly: ‘We remained in Australia for nearly two and a half years and Loughie did not change at all.’

On 22 May, Elizabeth, the new Duchess of York, recorded in her diary how Prince Edward had come to dinner where they were staying at Windsor and suggested they drive to London for an evening of dancing at the Embassy Club: ‘So we dashed up to London in his car & joined Paul’s party there. Among Prince Paul’s guests were the Prince of Wales’ friend Freda Dudley Ward, Sheila Loughborough, Alice Astor, Prince Serge Obolensky and Lord Cranborne. Danced hard till 2.30. David sent us back in his car. Very tired & enjoyed it awfully.’

Sheila posing as Penthesilea, Queen of the Amazons, in a photograph by Madame Yevonde, 1935

Sheila also made mention of the evening in her memoir, but from a different perspective: ‘The Prince of Wales was with us with Freda, of course, also the Duke and Duchess of York. Prince Bertie, now the Duke of York, had married the little debutante we had seen in the doorway…three years before, Elizabeth Bowes-Lyon.’

Sheila eventually divorced Loughie in 1926 and married Sir John Milbanke (‘Buffles’) in 1928. Sir John died in 1947 and in 1948 she opened a travel agency in Fortnum & Mason. In 1954, Sheila married Dimitri Romanov, a Russian prince. She died in 1969.

Prince Edward parted company from Freda Dudley Ward in 1934 when he met divorcée Mrs Wallis Simpson. He became King Edward VIII in January 1936 but abdicated in December of that year due to his determination to marry Wallis, which he did in 1937. That same year, his brother Prince Albert was crowned King George VI.
