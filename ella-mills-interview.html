Self-care rituals with Ella Mills

 Posted on August 1, 2024  |  7 minutes  |  1472 words  |  Jenniffer Sheldon

In our new series, Bazaar’s multiplatform beauty director speaks to inspirers in the wellness world about the rituals and routines that work for them. Here, Bridget March meets Ella Mills, the founder of Deliciously Ella – which over the course of 12 years has grown from a recipe blog to a brand whose revenue is estimated to be £20 million. As an iconic figure in today's global wellness phenomenon, Mills shares her personal journey of self-care, revealing what works for her now.

The Deliciously Ella mission to help people “eat real food again” stems from a very personal place, Ella Mills tells me. Her brand – now a household name – began in 2012 with a recipe blog teaching herself to cook. “I got ill with a condition that impaired the functioning of my autoimmune system,” she tells me, comparing it to the feel of long Covid, “in the sense that everything was wrong and no one really knew why, or what to do about it.” It affected her heart rate, digestion and circulation; she had chronic fatigue, brain fog, and all-over body pain – yet the medication prescribed didn’t help. Pulling herself out of “a very, very dark place,” Mills read others’ stories relating to poor health conditions and noticed a common thread correlating health with diet. It inspired her.

Parking things like supermarket sandwiches “with 35 ingredients in them”, she began preparing meals consisting of simple plant-based ingredients. Gradually, over the course of two years, Mills regained her health. “It wasn’t overnight, but I slowly came off all my medication,” she recalls, crediting her diet as the cure. Meanwhile, Deliciously Ella exploded. “I had about 130 million hits over those few years and that all led to my first cookbook coming out in 2015.” It was the fastest-selling debut cookbook ever, with six follow-ups – her latest being Healthy Made Simple, which resonates with what she’s now most proud of, professionally speaking.

Sure, there’s the success: alongside Matthew, her husband and business partner, Mills has sold one-and-a-half million books; developed more than 100 products sold in over 2,000 stores across 40 countries; accumulated four-million followers on social media and a 100-person team; opened cafés which evolved into a restaurant; and launched a successful app and podcast series. “But ultimately, it's the impact that the business has on people’s health that is the thing I'm most proud of,” she says, recounting feedback she receives daily.

I wonder, what does it all boil down to? “The thing we get most wrong about health and wellness is that it has to be complicated,” she offers. “That it must be all or nothing, or that we need to be doing quite niche things.” Removing those ideas “and bringing it back to the absolute basics,” is what works, she says.

As for how wellness looks for Mills in her day-to-day life today, here she shares the routines she's honed and the habits she's formed that continue to improve her health.

On her morning routine…

“I've got two little girls aged three and four. So, as you can imagine, between having small children and a business, it's a busy life. Something that helps me is getting up 45-to-60 minutes before them; it’s become the most important foundation for me to keep my health up.

“In this time I’ll shower, drink coffee, listen to my favourite music and read the newspaper or do a sudoku puzzle. I'll also do a 10-to-15-minute meditation. It's a daily-ish practice and I like this ‘ish’ because obviously it doesn’t happen 365 days a year; sometimes I simply don't want to.

“I feel like the day unfolds very quickly from there on until the kids are in bed. And so that is a really massive part of self-care for me, carving out some ‘me time’.”

Courtesy of Deliciously Ella

On small habits adding up…

“When you end up having multiple big responsibilities, spaces for self-care shrink – yet it’s when you most need to feel better in order to juggle it all. I think that's been one of the big lessons over the last few years, that it's easy to let perfect be the enemy of good; to say ‘I'm not going to do anything because I can’t do everything’.

“So, swapping the thought that ‘I can't go to the gym, so I won't exercise’ to ‘I'm going to go for a 20-minute walk on my lunch break’, or ‘I'm going to do a 10-minute yoga class at home on my bedroom floor’. I'm consistently surprised by the difference that five or 10 minutes of something can make to your health. And if you keep adding those up over time, they have such a profound impact.

“I also find batch-cooking on Sundays helpful, so I cook a meal and have loads left over for the fridge or freezer. It takes an extra five minutes, but it means I've got one extra meal for my husband and two extra meals for the kids. I also do lots of prep-ahead porridges, where I just mix all the ingredients together the night before. In the morning rush I then know we can all eat something healthy.”

On her approach to exercise…

“I've been trying to do a reformer Pilates class once or twice a week. My posture was so terrible after my kids were born and it’s been amazing for that. I also try to walk a lot – I walk the kids to school and back and into the office, so I get a lot of steps in.

“I did my yoga teacher training back in 2018. I love the whole philosophy around the practice. I do that mostly at home and the kids do it with me, which is really sweet and fun. I’m just trying to embrace the chaos instead of saying, ‘I can't do anything, because I've got them with me’.

“In an ideal world, I would have more time to do a bit more, but this chapter of my life doesn't look like that. I really believe in looking at everything in chapters. I might not do everything I want to at this point, and that's okay. In a few years’ time it will be a bit more straightforward.”

On her evening routine…

“I cook almost every evening, which I used to see as a chore but that’s something I have tried to flip. I now use the time to listen to a podcast or music, or call my mum or dad – it’s downtime as opposed to another thing on the to-do list. I found that mindset shift quite helpful.

“My husband and I have dinner, which I really enjoy, and then I often end up working for a bit, catching up on emails – but afterwards I try to leave my phone downstairs.

“I love reading so I always have a book on the go before bed. I just finished Tomorrow, and Tomorrow, and Tomorrow, which 100 per cent lives up to the hype. I thought it was brilliant.

“I'm also obsessed with skincare. I do a 100-step routine with a face mask and all the rest of it. I like the symbolism of investing in yourself and giving yourself that time.

“Recently I've been doing a five-to-10-minute pre-bed meditation guided by my app, which helps me sleep so much better.”

On the best healthy investments…

“I would say a great yoga mat does make a difference, I've had the same Liforme yoga mat now for 10 years, so it was definitely a great investment.

“I also love the Nutribullet – I think that's the kitchen item I use the most. It’s small and simple, it doesn't take up much space, but it's fantastic.

“But I think there's some danger in people feeling like they should spend money on various different supplements, gadgets, gizmos – it can also make the world of health and wellness feel really inaccessible to some people. We need to shift health and wellness back to very simple things.

“Ask yourself, ‘what is the one thing I can do today to improve my health?’ That might be making a 15-minute stir-fry, that might be going for a walk on your lunch break without your phone, that might be doing a quick yoga class or meditation – it doesn't need to be all of it. Do one thing each day to feel healthier, to feel happier. And then try to bring consistency to that. It can just be small, simple building blocks.”
