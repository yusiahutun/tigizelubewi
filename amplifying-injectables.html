Boosting Botox - Botox and Fillers tips

 Posted on May 1, 2024  |  5 minutes  |  1055 words  |  Larita Shotwell

Call it the youthful face holy grail: Everyone wants smooth, lifted, and wrinkle-free skin.

And thanks to the non-invasive magic of line-erasing neurotoxins and face-plumping hyaluronic acid, getting it is easier than ever. But with prices ranging between $300 and $1,200 for Botox and up to $2,500 for fillers, they can be a hefty investment.

The good news is there are things you can do to extend the life of your injections so you can schedule fewer doctor’s visits and spend less in the long run. Here, experts reveal their best tips on what to do pre- and post- procedure and how a high-tech facial gadget can stretch that youthful glow in between appointments.

Your In-Between Routine

Be Strategic About Moisturizer

“If Botox and fillers are like exercise, then facial products are like a healthy diet,” says Dana Goldberg, M.D. a plastic surgeon in West Palm Beach, Florida. “You can get a good result with one, but focusing on both will always be better.” Supercharge your skincare in the weeks and months following your injection by incorporating a moisturizer with ultra-hydrating ingredients like glycerin and hyaluronic acid, says Noëlle S. Sherber, M.D., a dermatologist in Washington D.C. It will “dovetail off the internal hydrating effects of the injected hyaluronic acid fillers and enhance the skin-smoothing results of Botox."

Boost Collagen with Retinol

Nothing helps compound the collagen-boosting benefits of fillers over time more than retinol, says Dr. Sherber. It will also help the surface of your skin remain plump and glowing. For faster results, ask your dermatologist for a prescription. If your skin is on the sensitive side, go with an over-the-counter version.

Firm Up with a Facial Device

Investing in an at-home microcurrent device, like the ReFa S Carat, $158, which is designed to emit low-level electrical currents to stimulate skin cells and accelerate cellular repair, can help “tone and firm up facial muscles, while improving the texture of your skin, and may even complement the reduction in lines provided by Botox,” says Los Angeles dermatologist Sonia Batra, M.D.. In fact, clinical studies have shown that after twenty days of microcurrent treatments, collagen and elastin production increases and blood circulation improves, which means patients may actually need less filler in the future, says Dr. Goldberg.

Get Serious About Sun Protection

In the quest to look better, broad-spectrum SPF is a non-negotiable. “UV damage is the number one cause of aging skin,” says Dr. Goldberg. She adds: “Botox and fillers can target specific areas of the face, but the overall appearance of the skin is also important in how we look.” To troubleshoot a host of issues, including loss of elasticity, pigment changes, and wrinkle formation, apply a sunscreen of at least SPF 30 daily.

Before Your Next Injection

Stop Taking Certain Medications

“I advise my patients to discontinue non-steroidal anti-inflammatories, as well as fish oil supplements and the four Gs—gingko, ginger, garlic, and ginseng—two weeks prior to the procedure unless medically necessary,” says Dr. Sherber. The reason? They all thin the blood and inhibit clotting, which can increase your risk of developing a bruise post-procedure. For pain relief leading up to your appointment, acetaminophen is your best bet.

Back Off the Booze

As tempting as a glass of wine may be to help calm your nerves, alcohol is a no-go a few days before an injection, says Dr. Batra. “It also acts as a blood thinner, making you more susceptible to additional downtime.” The trade-off is well worth it, though, in the form of a lot less bleeding, bruising, and swelling during and after your treatment.

Say No to Salt

Cut back at least 24 hours before your appointment, especially if you’re getting dermal fillers. "Injectable fillers are made from hyaluronic acid, which pulls in water, so loading up on salty foods beforehand can make you look more swollen the day after your procedure,” explains Dr. Sherber. And remember: The less bruising and swelling you have, the more under the radar your treatment will be.

Curb Bruising
If you tend to bruise easily, ask your doctor to ice each area that’s being treated for a few minutes before the actual injection. “It helps constrict the blood vessels and decreases the chance of discoloration afterwards,” says Dr. Batra. If you’ve got hard-to-see veins, an infrared light device like AccuVein, can help your doctor spot them beforehand to avoid hitting them with a needle and causing bruising later on. In the case of fillers, your doctor can use a blunt-tip cannula (rather than a traditional needle), which Dr. Batra says is more accurate and tends to cause fewer contusions.

After Your Injection

Ice Every Hour

With Botox, expect to be a little red and blotchy for about 10 minutes post-procedure; fillers will leave you puffy and swollen for the next couple of days, depending on the number of shots you get. Icing for a few minutes every hour will help temper any irritation. Just be sure to place a clean paper towel underneath your ice pack, advises Dr. Sherber, since “tiny openings through which bacteria can enter are created where each needle pricks the skin.” Also, it’s best to go makeup-free for the remainder of the day, but if you have to look presentable, apply cosmetics with a clean brush or freshly-washed fingertips.

Treat skin with care

Be extra gentle when applying skincare products and steer clear of retinol, glycolic acid, and exfoliating agents for 24 to 48 hours post-treatment to minimize the risk of irritation, says Dr. Goldberg. Don’t use anything harsh like a cleansing brush or facial device, says Heidi Waldorf, M.D. an associate clinical professor of dermatology at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai in New York. “Most fillers need at least two weeks to settle, so you don’t [want to] disrupt or interfere before it does.”

Speed Healing

To prevent bruising following your procedure, apply a topical arnica gel or an arnica-infused patch for the next two to three days. Dr. Batra also recommends icing every couple of hours for five to ten minutes and taking oral bromelain supplements (500mg twice daily for three days), to help decrease inflammation and speed healing. And since exposure to heat can open up blood vessels and make bruising worse, avoid strenuous exercise, saunas, steam rooms, and hot showers for at least 48 hours post-injection.
